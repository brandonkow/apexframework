## 1. Decision framework

Frame the study around decisions rather than generic market description:

- whether to proceed;
- target buyer and occupier;
- product and title format;
- development scale and density;
- unit mix and saleable areas;
- net selling price and price per area;
- expected sales velocity;
- launch window and phasing;
- downside risks and intervention triggers.

Use a one-sentence decision statement:

> Determine whether the subject development can support the proposed scale, pricing, and sales pace, and recommend the target market, product, unit mix, net price, phasing, and risk controls.

## 2. Geographic framework

Define five levels:

1. **Macro region:** metropolitan or economic region.
2. **Jurisdiction:** city, state, or local-authority boundary used by official sources.
3. **Submarkets:** buyer-recognized districts, neighborhoods, or corridors.
4. **Site catchment:** distance and travel-time areas around the subject site.
5. **Competitive market:** projects seeking the same buyer, regardless of boundary.

Define submarkets using buyer substitution, accessibility, employment, amenities, product, tenure, and price. Do not rely only on administrative convenience.

Prepare maps for regional context, site accessibility, supply and pipeline, and comparables.

## 3. Analytical stages

### 3.1 Demand drivers

Analyse:

- population and household formation;
- household size and age profile;
- employment and major job centers;
- income and mortgage affordability;
- migration, expatriate, student, medical, or retirement demand where relevant;
- transport, infrastructure, education, retail, health, and lifestyle catchments;
- rental demand and tenant profile.

Output target-buyer hypotheses and affordability bands. State whether demand is owner-occupier, investor, temporary-occupancy, or mixed.

### 3.2 Existing supply

Analyse stock by year, geography, product type, tenure, age, scale, and density. Explain structural reasons such as planning intensity, land scarcity, accessibility, and established buyer preference.

Required outputs:

- historical stock trend;
- product composition;
- geographic concentration;
- existing-stock age and replacement opportunity;
- high-rise acceptance in the catchment.

### 3.3 Future supply

Separate incoming, under-construction, approved, and announced projects. Analyse completion timing, product, geography, tenure, buyer segment, price position, and delivery confidence.

Calculate:

```text
Pipeline pressure = Future units / Existing stock
Direct competitive supply = Pipeline units seeking the same buyer and price band
Annual completion burden = Units expected to complete in each year
```

Test both launch-period and completion-period competition.

### 3.4 Transactions

Analyse annual and recent-period volume and value by geography, market type, product, price band, unit size, and bedroom count.

Distinguish:

- market growth caused by more transactions;
- price growth without liquidity growth;
- primary-market activity;
- secondary-market liquidity;
- owner-occupier versus investment signals.

Compare only like-for-like periods. Never compare a partial year with a full year. Tag aggregate records with period start, period end, coverage, and a comparison group such as `full-year`, `h1`, or `q1`.

### 3.5 Pricing, affordability, and rental

Use median and distribution evidence rather than relying only on averages. Separate gross developer price, net developer price, asking price, and achieved transaction price.

Calculate:

```text
Net effective price psf = Net effective price / Saleable area
Primary premium = New-project net psf / Comparable secondary psf - 1
Gross rental yield = Annual gross rent / Purchase price
Affordability ratio = Typical unit price / Target household annual income
```

Explain price differences through location, age, tenure, unit size, floor, view, specification, brand, and commercial incentives.

### 3.6 Overhang and inventory

Use the applicable official overhang definition. Do not treat all unsold units as official overhang.

Analyse by geography, product, price, size, completion status, inventory age, and project. Calculate remaining months of inventory where reliable recent velocity exists.

Identify product characteristics associated with slow absorption. Do not assume low price means low risk.

### 3.7 Secondary comparables

Use completed and transacting projects to establish achieved price support, rental evidence, occupancy, and buyer acceptance. Control for age, condition, tenure, size, and location.

### 3.8 New-launch comparables

Record:

- developer and location;
- tenure and title;
- blocks, storeys, and total units;
- units per floor, lifts, and parking;
- layouts, bedroom mix, and sizes;
- facilities and specification;
- launch and completion dates;
- units released, verified sales, bookings, and remaining stock;
- gross price, net price, and price per area;
- incentives, furnishing, maintenance, and sinking fund;
- target buyer, rental proposition, and buyer objections;
- source and survey date.

### 3.9 Absorption

Compare projects using time-normalized measures:

```text
Sales rate = Verified sales / Released units
Monthly sales velocity = Verified sales / Months since launch
Recent absorption = Sales in recent period / Months in recent period
Remaining months = Remaining units / Recent monthly absorption
Capture rate = Expected annual subject sales / Relevant annual market transactions
```

State denominators and release dates. Never present bookings as confirmed sales.

### 3.10 Comparable selection

Use four groups:

- **Direct:** closest buyer substitutes.
- **Aspirational:** stronger projects supporting a credible premium or differentiation.
- **Control:** similar but underperforming projects exposing downside.
- **Contextual:** broader projects explaining market structure.

Score candidates transparently and document overrides. Test whether the recommendation changes when aspirational projects or outliers are removed.

### 3.11 Market gap

Build position matrices for:

- price versus size;
- total price versus bedrooms;
- price versus velocity;
- density versus performance;
- accessibility versus price;
- maintenance versus facilities;
- owner-occupier versus investor orientation;
- unit type versus remaining inventory.

For each apparent gap, test demand, affordability, site fit, buildability, margin, and evidence from failed or absent products. An empty matrix quadrant is not automatically an opportunity.

## 4. Market-cycle logic

Use multiple indicators:

| Evidence combination | Possible interpretation |
|---|---|
| Rising transactions, falling overhang, improving absorption | Recovery or expansion |
| Rising prices, declining transaction volume | Affordability pressure or supply constraint |
| Large pipeline, weak velocity, rising inventory | Oversupply risk |
| Stable transactions, varied project performance | Selective or segmented market |
| High bookings, low completed occupancy | Investor-led risk |
| Falling inventory, stable prices | Rebalancing |
| Strong secondary liquidity and rental demand | More resilient occupier base |

Classify submarkets and product segments separately. State the data supporting the label and what evidence would invalidate it.

## 5. Development recommendations

Quantify:

- decision status;
- primary and secondary target markets;
- product and title format;
- positioning tier;
- unit count and density range;
- unit mix and saleable-area schedule;
- net price and price-per-area range;
- expected monthly and annual sales;
- phase size and release sequence;
- launch and completion window;
- facilities, parking, lift, and operating-cost targets;
- product differentiation;
- base, upside, and downside cases;
- monitoring and intervention triggers.

Use an evidence-to-decision matrix:

| Evidence | Decision influenced |
|---|---|
| Existing and future supply | Scale, density, phasing |
| Transaction price bands | Total price and buyer depth |
| Secondary transactions | Achieved-price foundation |
| New-project net prices | Launch position and premium |
| Overhang and inventory | Product and price exposure to avoid |
| Sales velocity | Phase size and release schedule |
| Rental and occupancy | Investor proposition |
| Affordability | Unit size and financing fit |
| Buyer interviews | Layout, facilities, and marketing |

## 6. Drafting order

Draft in this order:

1. reconcile the evidence store;
2. calculate deterministic metrics;
3. produce tables and chart specifications;
4. approve or validate the comparable basket;
5. complete project profiles;
6. write chart commentary;
7. prepare positioning and market-gap analysis;
8. write trends, cycle, and outlook;
9. write development recommendations and scenarios;
10. write the executive summary last;
11. red-team, validate, and issue.

Write analytical commentary as observation, explanation, implication, and caveat.

## 7. Report structure

Use this main-body sequence:

1. Executive decision summary.
2. Assignment, site, scope, and limitations.
3. Geography and site context.
4. Demand drivers and target buyers.
5. Market key indicators.
6. Existing supply.
7. Future supply.
8. Transactions.
9. Pricing, affordability, rental, and yield.
10. Overhang and inventory.
11. Secondary-market benchmark.
12. New-project comparables.
13. Sales velocity and absorption.
14. Product and positioning gap.
15. Trends, cycle, and outlook.
16. Development strategy.
17. Scenarios, risks, and triggers.
18. Conclusion.

Place detailed profiles, source registers, full transactions, definitions, calculations, and interview records in appendices.
